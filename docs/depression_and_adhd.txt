DEPRESSION AND ADHD — UNDERSTANDING LOW MOOD AND MOTIVATION
============================================================

Depression and ADHD frequently co-occur. Research suggests that 30–50% of people with ADHD will experience a depressive episode at some point. Understanding how they interact is important because they can look similar but respond to different approaches.

WHAT DEPRESSION FEELS LIKE
-----------------------------
- Persistent low mood or emptiness that doesn't lift — often for weeks at a time
- Loss of interest in things that usually matter (hobbies, relationships, food)
- Deep fatigue that sleep does not fix
- Feelings of worthlessness, guilt, or shame that feel like facts, not feelings
- Difficulty concentrating (overlaps with ADHD)
- Moving or speaking more slowly than usual
- In some people, irritability rather than sadness
- Thoughts of not wanting to be here, or that things would be easier if you weren't

ADHD AND DEPRESSION: HOW THEY INTERWEAVE
------------------------------------------
ADHD can cause depression. Years of struggling, failing, being criticised, and falling behind relative to peers creates accumulated grief and shame. This is sometimes called "ADHD-related depression" — it grows out of the pain of living undiagnosed or unsupported.

Key differences to know:

ADHD motivation problems:
- Can shift with novelty, urgency, interest, or challenge
- You might feel flat about one thing but energised about another in the same day
- The "paralysis" often lifts when something genuinely interests you

Depression motivation problems:
- Flatter, more uniform — hard to care about anything, even things that used to matter
- Even novelty often doesn't help
- The low mood is more pervasive, lasting weeks, not just a bad day

In practice: many people have both, and they layer on each other. ADHD makes managing depression harder. Depression makes ADHD symptoms worse.

ADHD BURNOUT VS DEPRESSION
----------------------------
ADHD burnout is increasingly recognised as a specific pattern:
- Comes after long periods of masking, overworking, or compensating for ADHD
- Feels like shutdown: difficulty getting out of bed, inability to do basic tasks
- Emotional numbness or extreme emotional sensitivity
- Feeling completely empty or hollow
- Often triggered by a major demand period (exams, new job, relationship conflict)

Burnout recovery typically requires: rest, reducing demands, reconnecting with safe pleasures, and time.

Depression recovery often benefits from: therapeutic support, gradually increasing activity, addressing thought patterns, and sometimes medication.

The two can overlap significantly. A good therapist or psychiatrist can help distinguish them.

SHAME CYCLES WITH ADHD
------------------------
A very common pattern:
1. ADHD symptom causes difficulty (missed deadline, forgotten commitment, messy space)
2. External criticism or self-criticism follows
3. Shame: "I am lazy / broken / not good enough"
4. Shame causes withdrawal and reduced activity
5. Reduced activity worsens ADHD and mood
6. Repeat

Breaking the shame cycle is often more important than "fixing" the behaviour. Self-compassion is not letting yourself off the hook — it is the emotional prerequisite for change.

BEHAVIORAL ACTIVATION FOR LOW MOOD
-------------------------------------
When motivation is very low, waiting to "feel like it" doesn't work. Behavioural activation reverses this:
- Action comes before motivation, not after
- Choose one tiny activity that used to bring even a small amount of pleasure
- Do it even if you don't want to — not to feel better immediately, but to interrupt inertia
- Examples: step outside for 5 minutes, make a cup of tea slowly, listen to one song

The goal is not "feeling good" — it is "not being completely still."

Tiny wins matter with ADHD and depression:
- Make the bed (or just fold the blanket)
- Text one person
- Eat something
- Shower (even if it takes 3 hours to start)
Each of these is genuinely worth acknowledging.

WHAT TO WATCH FOR: WHEN LOW MOOD NEEDS URGENT SUPPORT
-------------------------------------------------------
Some experiences require professional support as a priority:
- Thoughts of not wanting to be here, or of harming yourself
- Feeling like others would be better off without you
- Planning to hurt yourself
- Complete inability to function for several weeks

These are not signs of weakness. They are signs that the brain is in pain and needs help. Reaching out — even imperfectly — is the right thing to do.

SELF-COMPASSION PRACTICES FOR DEPRESSION WITH ADHD
----------------------------------------------------
Kristin Neff's three components of self-compassion:
1. Self-kindness: treating yourself the way you would treat a close friend who was struggling
2. Common humanity: recognising that suffering is part of being human — you are not alone in this
3. Mindfulness: seeing your pain clearly without drowning in it or pushing it away

A simple practice: Place one hand on your heart. Say quietly: "This is a moment of suffering. Suffering is part of life. May I be kind to myself right now."

This is not about positivity. It is about not abandoning yourself when things are hard.

WHAT HELPS WITH DEPRESSION AND ADHD
--------------------------------------
- Therapy (CBT or behavioural activation therapy) alongside ADHD strategies
- Light physical movement — even walking for 10 minutes has documented mood benefits
- Social connection — isolation deepens depression; even low-effort connection helps
- Sleep regulation — irregular sleep dramatically worsens both ADHD and depression
- Reducing alcohol — alcohol is a depressant and worsens mood significantly
- Professional support — a psychiatrist may consider medication if depression is persistent

WHAT DOESN'T HELP
-------------------
- Telling yourself to "just try harder" or "think positive"
- Comparing your productivity to others during a depressive episode
- Waiting until you feel ready to seek help
- Isolating completely (even though it is tempting)
- Staying in bed all day — this tends to worsen both ADHD and depression over time
