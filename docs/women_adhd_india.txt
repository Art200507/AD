WOMEN AND ADHD IN INDIA — A DIFFERENT EXPERIENCE
=================================================

ADHD in women and girls is systematically under-diagnosed and under-recognised. Understanding why — and what it actually looks like — is important for anyone who suspects they may have ADHD or has recently been diagnosed.

WHY WOMEN ARE DIAGNOSED LATER
--------------------------------
ADHD research has historically been conducted primarily on boys. The picture of ADHD that became embedded in medical culture — hyperactive, disruptive, bouncing off walls — describes a pattern more commonly seen in boys.

Girls with ADHD often present differently:
- Inattentive type is more common in girls: daydreaming, losing things, drifting off, forgetting
- Hyperactivity is often internal: a mind that never stops, a buzzing restlessness, racing thoughts
- Less overt disruption — girls often learn to internalise rather than externalise
- Compensating behaviors: working twice as hard to appear "normal," relying on intelligence to cover executive function gaps

The result: ADHD in girls is often misread, missed, or misdiagnosed as anxiety, depression, "just being sensitive," or simply not noticed at all.

Average age of diagnosis for women is significantly later than for men. Many women are diagnosed in their late 20s, 30s, or even later — often after reading about ADHD and recognising themselves, or after a child is diagnosed.

THE EXHAUSTION OF MASKING
--------------------------
Masking is the process of mimicking neurotypical behaviour — learning to perform normality in social situations by copying how others act, suppressing impulsive responses, monitoring yourself constantly.

Women with ADHD often become expert maskers:
- Smiling and making eye contact even when struggling to track the conversation
- Writing everything down in detailed notes to compensate for poor working memory
- Arriving early to avoid the shame of lateness
- Completing tasks at the last possible moment, but on time — so nobody sees the chaos underneath

What masking looks like from inside:
- "I perform normal all day and collapse when I get home."
- "People think I have it together. They have no idea."
- "By the end of the day I have nothing left."
- "I can hold things together publicly but my home / room / personal life is chaos."

Masking is enormously energy-intensive. It depletes the executive function reserves needed for everything else. Many women with ADHD experience ADHD burnout — not laziness, but genuine depletion from years of effort that was invisible to others.

THE "GOOD GIRL" MASK
---------------------
In Indian culture especially, girls are often socialised to be:
- Compliant and accommodating
- Emotionally available to others
- Academically diligent and not a "problem child"
- Not too loud, not too disruptive, not too much

This socialisation pressures girls with ADHD to suppress their symptoms from very early on. The hyperactive girl who can't sit still learns to sit still — at enormous cost. The girl who forgets everything learns elaborate systems to hide it. The girl who feels everything too intensely learns to perform composure.

The result is a presentation that looks controlled on the outside while being overwhelmed on the inside. And because there is no "visible problem," no one asks if she's okay.

EMOTIONAL EXPERIENCE OF ADHD IN WOMEN
----------------------------------------

Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD):
- Intense, sudden emotional pain triggered by perceived rejection, criticism, or failure
- Can feel like heartbreak from a neutral comment
- Can cause people-pleasing, over-apologising, avoiding situations where rejection is possible
- Often mistaken for anxiety, borderline traits, or "being too sensitive"

Emotional overwhelm:
- Emotional dysregulation is common in ADHD — feelings arrive fast and intensely
- Difficulty describing what you're feeling (alexithymia is common)
- May shut down, cry without knowing why, or feel emotions in the body without mental clarity
- This is a neurological pattern, not a character weakness

Internalized shame:
- Many women with ADHD have been told they are "too much" — too emotional, too scattered, too intense
- Or "not enough" — not disciplined enough, not organised enough, not trying enough
- The result is a background sense of being fundamentally flawed

HORMONES AND ADHD
------------------
Oestrogen has a direct impact on dopamine function — and therefore on ADHD symptoms.

Menstrual cycle:
- ADHD symptoms often worsen in the week before a period (when oestrogen drops sharply)
- This is sometimes called "ADHD PMS" — a week of significantly reduced functioning, worsened focus, more emotional dysregulation, lower distress tolerance
- This is real, biological, and not imagined
- Tracking your cycle alongside your symptoms often reveals this pattern clearly

Puberty:
- Rising then fluctuating oestrogen during puberty can dramatically worsen ADHD symptoms
- This is often when girls first begin to noticeably struggle, even if things seemed manageable in primary school

Postpartum:
- The drop in oestrogen after childbirth can trigger a significant worsening of ADHD symptoms
- Combined with sleep deprivation and the demands of new parenthood, this can be a very difficult period

Perimenopause:
- As oestrogen levels decline in the 40s-50s, ADHD symptoms often worsen significantly
- Women who were previously managing well may begin to struggle

AFTER DIAGNOSIS: WHAT TO EXPECT
----------------------------------
The period following an ADHD diagnosis often involves:

Relief: Finally having a framework for why things have been so hard.

Grief: For the years of struggling without support. For the relationships and opportunities affected. For the energy spent masking.

Anger: At the systems that missed this. At the people who called it laziness or sensitivity. At a medical world that didn't look for this in girls.

Reframing: Revisiting your past through a new lens — not to make excuses, but to give yourself understanding and compassion.

Questions about identity: "What is ADHD and what is me? Am I allowed to use ADHD to explain things? Am I being 'too much' again by talking about this?"

All of these are part of a natural process. There is no right way to process a diagnosis. It takes time.

NAVIGATING INDIAN FAMILY EXPECTATIONS WITH ADHD
-------------------------------------------------

Common family dynamics for women with ADHD in India:
- Being expected to manage household tasks, relationships, and emotional labour flawlessly
- Symptoms being read as carelessness, lack of respect, or "not caring about the family"
- ADHD diagnosis being dismissed: "Everyone is like that." "You don't need a label." "In our time we just managed."
- The "good daughter-in-law" standard intersecting with executive function deficits

Navigating this without adequate family understanding is exhausting. Some approaches:
- Sharing articles or resources about ADHD in women (in Hindi if that's more accessible)
- Framing ADHD in terms of brain functioning rather than behaviour
- Finding one person in the family who can be an ally, rather than needing everyone to understand
- Accepting that some family members may never understand, and protecting your energy accordingly

For many women, peer communities of other women with ADHD (online or in person) become the understanding that family cannot provide. This community is genuinely important.

YOU ARE NOT "TOO MUCH"
------------------------
You have heard this message in different forms throughout your life — from teachers, family, partners, your own inner voice.

You are not too much. You were in contexts that were too small for you.

Your intensity is not a problem. Your emotions are real information, not performance. Your brain works differently, and that is not a character flaw. The challenges are real — and so are the strengths. Both can be true at once.
