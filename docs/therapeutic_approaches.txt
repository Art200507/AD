THERAPEUTIC APPROACHES AND TECHNIQUES
======================================

This document outlines the therapeutic frameworks and conversation techniques used in effective mental health support. These approaches inform how a caring, skilled therapist responds to someone in emotional pain.

CORE CONDITIONS OF EFFECTIVE THERAPY (Carl Rogers)
----------------------------------------------------

Empathy:
- Genuinely trying to understand the world from the client's perspective
- Reflecting back what you hear with accuracy and care
- Not just intellectual understanding — felt understanding
- "What I'm hearing is that this has been incredibly lonely. Is that right?"

Unconditional Positive Regard:
- Accepting the person fully, without judgment or conditions
- Their thoughts, feelings, and experiences are all valid — even the ones they're ashamed of
- There is nothing they can say that makes them less worthy of care
- "That makes complete sense given what you've been through."

Genuineness (Congruence):
- Being real and present, not performing a role
- Warmth that is authentic, not performed
- Gently honest rather than artificially reassuring

ACTIVE LISTENING SKILLS
------------------------

Reflecting:
- Echoing back the emotional content of what was said
- "So what I'm hearing is that you felt completely invisible in that moment."
- This shows you heard the feeling, not just the facts

Summarising:
- Pulling together multiple things that were shared
- "You've shared a lot — let me see if I've understood. It sounds like the frustration about work has been building for a while, and what happened today just made it overflow. Does that feel right?"

Open questions:
- Questions that cannot be answered with yes/no
- "What was that like for you?"
- "How did that sit with you?"
- "What do you think is underneath that feeling?"
- "What would it mean if that were true?"

Socratic questioning:
- Questions that gently probe assumptions without attacking them
- "You said you're a failure. What would that actually need to be true for that to mean?"
- "Is there any evidence that doesn't fit that story?"
- "What would you say to a friend who said that about themselves?"

Naming emotions:
- Offering language for what someone is experiencing
- "It sounds like there's a lot of grief in what you're describing."
- "That sounds really lonely."
- "I wonder if some of what you're feeling is anger? Does that land?"

Checking in:
- Not assuming — always asking
- "What kind of support would be most helpful right now?"
- "Do you want me to just listen, or would it help to think through some options together?"
- "Is there anything in particular you were hoping to get from talking today?"

VALIDATION TECHNIQUES
----------------------

Validation is not agreement — it is acknowledging that a feeling makes sense given someone's experience.

Levels of validation:
1. Being present and listening — not interrupting, not fixing
2. Accurate reflection: "That sounds really hard."
3. Mind-reading warmly: "Of course that hurt — anyone would feel that way."
4. Connecting feeling to history: "Given everything you've been through, it makes complete sense that you'd feel that way."
5. Radical genuineness: "I get it. That would shake me too."

Phrases that validate without minimising:
- "That makes complete sense."
- "Of course you felt that way."
- "That sounds really exhausting."
- "I can understand why that hit so hard."
- "You're carrying a lot right now."

What NOT to say (invalidating responses):
- "At least..." (comparative minimising)
- "But think of the positives."
- "Other people have it worse."
- "You just need to..."
- "Why don't you just..."
- "I'm sure it's not that bad."
- "You're being too sensitive."

COGNITIVE BEHAVIOURAL TECHNIQUES
----------------------------------

Thought identification:
- Helping someone notice and articulate automatic thoughts
- "When that happened, what was the first thought that went through your mind?"
- "If there was a word or phrase that captured what you were thinking in that moment, what would it be?"

Cognitive distortions (patterns to gently explore):
- Catastrophising: assuming the worst possible outcome is certain
- All-or-nothing thinking: "I completely failed" when there were partial successes
- Mind-reading: assuming you know what others are thinking about you
- Personalising: taking responsibility for things outside your control
- Emotional reasoning: "I feel like a failure, therefore I am a failure"
- Filtering: focusing only on negatives while filtering out positives

Thought testing (gentle Socratic):
- "What's the evidence that this thought is true? What's the evidence against it?"
- "Is there another way of looking at this situation?"
- "What would you say to a friend who had this thought?"
- "How likely is the worst case, really? And if it did happen, could you survive it?"

Behavioural experiments:
- Testing anxious predictions by gently approaching feared situations
- Starting very small and building up
- "What's the smallest step you could take that would feel manageable?"

ACCEPTANCE AND COMMITMENT THERAPY (ACT) TECHNIQUES
----------------------------------------------------

Defusion (separating from thoughts):
- Thoughts are not facts — they are mental events
- "I notice I'm having the thought that I'm a failure." (vs "I am a failure")
- "My mind is telling me that..."
- This creates distance without fighting the thought

Acceptance of emotion:
- Not suppressing or avoiding difficult feelings, but making room for them
- "Where do you feel that in your body?"
- "Can you just notice it without trying to change it? What shape is it? What texture?"
- Emotions that are allowed to be there tend to move. Emotions that are suppressed intensify.

Values clarification:
- "If you imagine yourself 10 years from now, looking back — what would you want to have stood for in your life?"
- "What kind of person do you want to be? In your relationships, in how you treat yourself?"
- "What matters most to you, separate from what you 'should' do?"

Committed action:
- Taking small steps toward valued directions, even in the presence of difficult feelings
- "You don't have to feel ready. What is the one small thing you could do today that moves toward what matters to you?"

DIALECTICAL BEHAVIOUR THERAPY (DBT) SKILLS
--------------------------------------------

TIPP (for acute emotional distress):
- Temperature: cold water on face, ice cubes — activates the dive reflex, slows heart rate rapidly
- Intense exercise: even 2 minutes of vigorous movement discharges adrenaline
- Paced breathing: breathing out longer than you breathe in (in for 4, out for 6-8)
- Progressive muscle relaxation: tense and release each muscle group systematically

Opposite Action (for emotion regulation):
- When an emotion is driving an unhelpful urge, do the opposite
- Shame says hide → Opposite Action: reach out to someone safe
- Anxiety says avoid → Opposite Action: approach the feared thing (gently)
- Depression says withdraw → Opposite Action: do one small connecting thing

GIVE (for interpersonal effectiveness):
- Gentle (no attacks or judgment)
- Interested (listen to the other person)
- Validate (acknowledge their perspective)
- Easy manner (lighten up where possible)

DEAR MAN (for assertive requests):
- Describe the situation factually
- Express your feeling about it
- Assert what you need
- Reinforce why it benefits both of you
- Mindful (stay focused, don't get derailed)
- Appear confident
- Negotiate

TRAUMA-INFORMED RESPONSES
---------------------------

Safety first:
- Always establish emotional safety before exploring difficult material
- "We can go as slowly as you need."
- "You can stop at any point."
- Never push someone into difficult content before they feel safe

Window of tolerance:
- The zone in which someone can process difficult material without being overwhelmed
- Signs of going outside the window: shutting down, dissociating, flooding (overwhelm)
- Grounding when overwhelmed: "Let's just pause here. Can you feel your feet on the floor?"

Titration:
- Approaching difficult material in very small doses
- "We don't need to go all the way into that today. What's just a little bit you can share?"

Normalising responses to trauma:
- "What you're describing are completely normal responses to abnormal experiences."
- "Your nervous system learned to do this to protect you. It makes sense."

HOLDING DIFFICULTY WITHOUT FIXING IT
--------------------------------------
Not everything needs a solution. Sometimes the most therapeutic thing is to simply be present with someone's pain without rushing to make it better.

"I'm just going to sit with you in this for a moment."

Premature problem-solving can feel like the therapist is uncomfortable with pain and wants it to stop. Staying present communicates: your pain is okay to feel. I'm not afraid of it. You can feel it here.

Ask before advising:
- "Would it be helpful if I shared some ideas, or do you just need to be heard right now?"
- This simple question respects autonomy and prevents unwanted advice

The goal of therapy is not to fix the person — it is to help them develop a more compassionate, effective relationship with themselves and their experiences.
