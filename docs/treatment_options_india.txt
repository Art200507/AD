UNDERSTANDING TREATMENT OPTIONS FOR MENTAL HEALTH IN INDIA
===========================================================

Knowing what kind of help exists — and what to expect from it — makes reaching out much less overwhelming.

TYPES OF MENTAL HEALTH PROFESSIONALS
--------------------------------------

Psychiatrist:
- A medical doctor (MBBS + MD/DNB in Psychiatry)
- The only mental health professional who can prescribe medication in India
- Trained in diagnosis, medication management, and clinical assessment
- Does not usually provide weekly therapy — more focused on medication review and diagnosis
- First appointment is often longer (45–60 minutes), follow-ups shorter (15–30 minutes)
- Government hospitals have psychiatrists; private psychiatrists also exist

Clinical Psychologist:
- Holds an MPhil or PhD in Clinical Psychology, registered with the RCI (Rehabilitation Council of India)
- Provides therapy, psychological testing, and ADHD/learning disability assessments
- Cannot prescribe medication
- Typically works in longer sessions (50–60 minutes) over multiple weeks or months
- The person to look for if you want ongoing therapy

Counsellor / Psychotherapist:
- Training varies significantly — qualifications range from short courses to full master's programmes
- Provides talk-based support, often shorter-term
- Not RCI-regulated (unlike clinical psychologists), so credentials matter more here
- Can be a genuinely good source of support for mild to moderate concerns

TYPES OF THERAPY: WHAT THEY ARE AND WHEN THEY HELP
-----------------------------------------------------

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT):
- Explores the connection between thoughts, feelings, and behaviours
- Identifies unhelpful thinking patterns (catastrophising, all-or-nothing thinking, mind-reading) and tests them
- Gives practical tools: thought records, behavioural experiments, exposure
- Strongly evidence-based for anxiety, depression, and works well alongside ADHD
- Typically time-limited: 8–20 sessions

Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT):
- Originally developed for emotion regulation difficulties
- Teaches four skill sets: mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness
- Very useful for people with ADHD who struggle with intense emotions, impulsivity, and relationships
- Can be delivered individually or in skills groups

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT):
- Focuses on accepting difficult thoughts and feelings rather than fighting them
- Clarifies personal values and helps build a life aligned with them
- Particularly helpful for the shame and avoidance cycles common in ADHD
- Teaches "defusion" — seeing thoughts as just thoughts, not facts

Person-Centred / Supportive Therapy:
- Provides a warm, non-judgmental space to talk and feel heard
- No agenda or structured techniques — the therapist follows your lead
- Helpful when what you most need is to feel understood and not alone
- Often used at the beginning of therapy or as an ongoing support

Trauma-Informed Therapy:
- Approaches that specifically address trauma and its effects on the body and mind
- Includes EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing) — well-researched for trauma
- Important for people with ADHD who have accumulated trauma from years of criticism, failure, or difficult relationships

WHAT TO EXPECT FROM YOUR FIRST APPOINTMENT
--------------------------------------------

With a psychiatrist:
- They will ask about your symptoms, history, family history, sleep, and daily functioning
- They may use standardised assessment tools
- They may discuss a diagnosis, or say they need to see you again first
- They may suggest medication, therapy, or both
- You can ask questions: "What is this diagnosis?" "What are my options?" "What does this medication do?"

With a therapist or psychologist:
- The first session is usually an intake: they learn about you, you learn about them
- You can ask about their approach, experience with ADHD, and what therapy will look like
- It is normal for therapy to feel awkward at first — this usually passes
- You are allowed to change therapists if the fit does not feel right

ADHD ASSESSMENT IN INDIA
--------------------------
A formal ADHD diagnosis from a psychiatrist or clinical psychologist:
- Involves a detailed clinical interview about childhood, school, work, and relationships
- Standardised rating scales (Conners, DIVA, or similar) are often used
- Collateral information (from parents or partners) is sometimes requested
- A neuropsychological assessment may be conducted by a clinical psychologist to rule out other causes
- The process typically takes 1–3 appointments
- A written report can be issued if needed for accommodations at college or work

MEDICATION FOR ADHD IN INDIA
------------------------------
Two main categories are available:

Stimulant medication:
- Methylphenidate (brand names include Ritalin, Concerta, Inspiral)
- Requires a Schedule X prescription — more controlled than standard prescriptions
- Available at larger pharmacies and hospital pharmacies in major cities
- Can be harder to find in smaller towns or rural areas
- Works by increasing dopamine and norepinephrine availability in the brain

Non-stimulant medication:
- Atomoxetine (brand names include Axetra, Attentrol, Strattera)
- Easier to obtain — not Schedule X
- Takes 4–6 weeks to reach full effect (unlike stimulants, which work the same day)
- An option when stimulants are not suitable or available

Important to understand:
- Medication helps manage symptoms — it does not cure ADHD
- Many people with ADHD do not take medication and manage well with therapy and strategies
- Medication works best alongside therapy, structure, and self-understanding
- Side effects vary by person; a psychiatrist adjusts dosage over time
- Never stop prescribed medication abruptly without medical guidance

WHAT IF I CANNOT AFFORD THERAPY?
----------------------------------
Therapy is expensive in India, but options exist:

Government hospital OPDs:
- Psychiatry OPDs are free or cost ₹10–50 per consultation
- Available at every government hospital with a psychiatric department
- The option many people don't realise is genuinely accessible

Medical college psychiatry departments:
- Often have both psychiatry and clinical psychology services
- Supervised by qualified faculty; quality is generally good
- More affordable than private practice

iCall (TISS):
- Sliding scale — you pay what you can genuinely afford
- Worth contacting and explaining your financial situation honestly

Online peer support (free):
- Reddit (r/ADHD, r/IndianMentalHealth, r/adhdwomen) — anonymous, supportive communities
- Not a replacement for therapy, but a meaningful source of connection and validation

Self-help resources (free):
- CBT workbooks and DBT worksheets are freely available online
- Structured self-help alongside a supportive community can make a genuine difference while working toward professional support
